Purpose:
This file is an educational triage knowledge seed for retrieval-augmented responses. It summarizes common symptom patterns, urgent warning signs, and safe next-step guidance. It does not diagnose, prescribe, or replace clinician evaluation.

Reference policy:
The notes below are original summaries derived from reputable clinical references. No copyrighted textbook passages are copied. When answering, cite the reference title listed for the matching section.

General triage rules:
- If symptoms suggest immediate danger, recommend emergency care first.
- Red flags include severe chest pain, difficulty breathing, fainting, new neurologic weakness, confusion, severe allergic reaction, severe dehydration, uncontrolled bleeding, pregnancy with severe pain or bleeding, and sudden severe headache.
- For uncertain or incomplete symptoms, recommend clinician evaluation and ask focused follow-up questions.
- For mild symptoms without red flags, provide conservative self-care and monitoring advice while explaining when to seek care.

Symptom: chest pain
Key features to ask:
- Location, quality, onset, duration, radiation, exertional trigger, shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, fainting, cough, fever, injury, reflux symptoms, and cardiac risk factors.
Urgent warning signs:
- Crushing or pressure-like chest pain, pain radiating to arm/jaw/back, shortness of breath, sweating, fainting, new confusion, low blood pressure symptoms, or chest pain with neurologic symptoms.
Common possible categories:
- Cardiac ischemia or angina, pulmonary embolism, pneumonia, reflux/esophageal causes, chest wall strain, anxiety or panic, pericarditis, aortic emergency.
Safe recommendation:
- Treat high-risk chest pain as emergency until evaluated. Suggest emergency services for severe, persistent, exertional, radiating, or breathlessness-associated pain.

Symptom: headache
Key features to ask:
- Suddenness, worst-ever severity, fever, neck stiffness, neurologic symptoms, trauma, pregnancy/postpartum status, age over 50 with new headache, cancer/immunosuppression, vision changes, pattern change, medication use.
Urgent warning signs:
- Thunderclap onset, focal weakness/numbness, confusion, seizure, fever with stiff neck, head injury, papilledema/vision loss, or new headache in pregnancy/postpartum.
Common possible categories:
- Tension-type headache, migraine, sinus/viral illness, dehydration, medication overuse, meningitis, intracranial bleeding, stroke-related headache, temporal arteritis in older adults.
Safe recommendation:
- Emergency evaluation for red flags. For stable recurrent headaches without red flags, advise hydration, rest, avoidance of known triggers, and clinician follow-up if new, worsening, frequent, or disabling.
Reference:
- StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf: "Acute Headache" and "Chronic Headaches."
- URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK554510/
- URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK559083/

Symptom: abdominal pain
Key features to ask:
- Location, migration, onset, duration, severity, vomiting, diarrhea, blood in stool, fever, urinary symptoms, pregnancy possibility, prior surgery, trauma, appetite loss, abdominal swelling.
Urgent warning signs:
- Severe or worsening pain, rigid abdomen, rebound tenderness, persistent vomiting, fainting, black/bloody stool, fever with severe pain, pregnancy with pain/bleeding, right lower quadrant pain with fever/vomiting, jaundice with fever.
Common possible categories:
- Gastroenteritis, constipation, reflux/gastritis, gallbladder disease, appendicitis, kidney stone or urinary infection, pancreatitis, bowel obstruction, gynecologic causes.
Safe recommendation:
- Severe, localized, worsening, or systemic abdominal pain needs urgent evaluation. Mild short-lived pain without red flags may be monitored with hydration and bland foods, but persistent pain needs medical review.
Reference:
- StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf: "Acute Abdomen" and "Appendicitis."
- URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK459328/
- URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK493193/

Symptom: shortness of breath / dyspnea
Key features to ask:
- Onset, exertional or resting symptoms, chest pain, cough, fever, wheeze, leg swelling, orthopnea, history of asthma/COPD/heart disease, anemia symptoms, recent travel/surgery, pregnancy.
Urgent warning signs:
- Severe breathlessness, blue lips, inability to speak full sentences, chest pain, fainting, confusion, oxygen saturation concern, sudden onset with leg swelling/pain, or allergic swelling.
Common possible categories:
- Asthma/COPD flare, pneumonia, heart failure, pulmonary embolism, anemia, anxiety/panic, allergic reaction.
Safe recommendation:
- Sudden, severe, or chest-pain-associated dyspnea is urgent. Stable mild dyspnea still needs clinician assessment if new, recurrent, worsening, or associated with fever/cough/leg swelling.
Reference:
- Merck Manual Professional Edition: "Dyspnea."
- URL: https://www.merckmanuals.com/professional/pulmonary-disorders/symptoms-of-pulmonary-disorders/dyspnea

Symptom: cough
Key features to ask:
- Duration, fever, sputum, blood, chest pain, shortness of breath, wheeze, smoking/vaping, asthma/COPD, reflux/postnasal drip, exposure, weight loss/night sweats.
Urgent warning signs:
- Coughing blood, severe breathlessness, chest pain, high fever with worsening condition, confusion, dehydration, or risk factors for pulmonary embolism.
Common possible categories:
- Viral upper respiratory infection, bronchitis, pneumonia, asthma, COPD, postnasal drip, reflux, medication effect, tuberculosis or malignancy in selected contexts.
Safe recommendation:
- Emergency/urgent care for red flags. Persistent cough beyond several weeks, recurrent fever, blood, weight loss, or risk factors needs clinician evaluation.
Reference:
- StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf: "Cough: Evaluation and Management" and "Chronic Cough."
- URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK493221/
- URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK430791/

Symptom: dizziness / vertigo / imbalance
Key features to ask:
- Spinning versus lightheadedness, fainting, hearing loss/tinnitus, headache, neurologic symptoms, chest pain, palpitations, dehydration, medications, duration, positional trigger.
Urgent warning signs:
- New weakness/numbness, slurred speech, double vision, severe headache, inability to walk, chest pain, fainting, or persistent vomiting/dehydration.
Common possible categories:
- Benign positional vertigo, vestibular neuritis, migraine-associated vertigo, dehydration, medication effect, low blood pressure, arrhythmia, stroke/posterior circulation problem.
Safe recommendation:
- Dizziness with neurologic or cardiac symptoms is urgent. Positional brief vertigo without red flags can be outpatient, but persistent or recurrent symptoms need evaluation.
Reference:
- StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf: "Evaluation of the Dizzy and Unbalanced Patient" and "Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo."
- URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK589645/
- URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK470308/

Symptom: fever
Key features to ask:
- Temperature, duration, chills, cough, urinary symptoms, rash, neck stiffness, abdominal pain, travel, immune compromise, pregnancy, recent surgery, medications.
Urgent warning signs:
- Fever with confusion, stiff neck, severe headache, trouble breathing, persistent vomiting, dehydration, purple/nonblanching rash, immune compromise, very young infant, or severe worsening.
Common possible categories:
- Viral illness, bacterial infection, pneumonia, urinary infection, gastrointestinal infection, drug reaction, inflammatory illness.
Safe recommendation:
- Fever with red flags needs urgent care. Persistent fever, recurrent fever, or fever in high-risk patients needs clinician evaluation.
Reference:
- StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf: fever-related emergency and infectious disease evaluation topics.
- URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK570583/

Symptom: rash and visible skin changes
Key features to ask:
- Onset, itch/pain, fever, medication exposure, mucous membrane involvement, spread, blisters, facial/lip swelling, breathing symptoms, tick/insect exposure, immune status.
Urgent warning signs:
- Rash with trouble breathing, facial/tongue swelling, high fever, skin pain, blisters, peeling, purple/nonblanching spots, mucous membrane erosions, rapid spread, or severe illness.
Common possible categories:
- Viral rash, allergy/urticaria, eczema/dermatitis, cellulitis, drug eruption, shingles, serious blistering drug reaction in selected contexts.
Safe recommendation:
- Emergency care for allergic airway symptoms or severe systemic rash. New medication-associated rash with fever, mucosal lesions, pain, or peeling is urgent.
Reference:
- StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf: "Cutaneous Adverse Drug Reaction" and dermatology rash references.
- URL: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK533000/
